What Order Should You Teach Phonics? A Reading Roadmap for Parents

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Teach phonics in this order: letter sounds alongside letter names, uppercase and lowercase, hearing sounds inside words, consonants/vowels and short vowel sounds, blending CVC words (like cat and sun), and only then long vowels, silent e, digraphs, and blends. Each stage rests on the one before it, so mastery matters more than speed.

One of the most common questions I hear from parents is, "I want to help at home — but where do I even start?" The good news: reading isn't taught all at once. It's built one skill at a time, in a particular order, with each step resting on the one before it. This is the same roadmap I use with children every week in Williamson County, written out so you can follow along at home.

Before we start, one principle matters more than any single step: this age learns through repetition. Teach a skill until it's truly mastered before moving on, and keep circling back to it all year. A skill that feels "done" in October still needs a quick review in March. Slow and solid beats fast and shaky every time.

1. Letters and their sounds — together

Start by singing the alphabet daily — all year long. As you introduce new letters, teach the sound alongside the name, and add movement: sign language, a hand motion, or acting the sound out. Children this age learn with their whole bodies, so a letter they can sing, sign, and move to sticks far better than one they only see. A helpful tip: teach the consonants first, since they're the most predictable building blocks.

2. Uppercase, lowercase, and putting letters in order

Next, teach that there are two kinds of letters: uppercase and lowercase. Make it physical — have your child stand tall for an uppercase letter and squat down for a lowercase one. From there, work on putting the alphabet in order and matching lowercase letters to their uppercase partners. These feel like games, and that's exactly the point.

3. Hearing sounds inside words

This is the quiet hero of early reading. Long before fluent decoding, children need to hear the sounds in words — the beginning, middle, and ending sounds. Play games like, "Which two of these — sock, lock, cat — sound the same at the end?" or "What sound does sun start with?" This is an ongoing skill you'll keep practicing all year, and it's one of the strongest predictors of later reading success.

4. Consonants, vowels, and short vowel sounds

Now teach the second way letters divide: consonants and vowels. Clap out the vowels together — a, e, i, o, u — until your child can tell you whether a letter is a consonant or a vowel. This is also a natural moment to teach directionality: we read left to right. Move your bodies left to right as you say the words, so the habit lives in their hands as well as their eyes. Then begin the short vowel sounds, just one at a time.

5. Decoding and building CVC words

With a handful of consonants and short vowels in place, your child is ready for CVC words — consonant-vowel-consonant words like cat, sun, pig. Show them how to push the sounds together: /c/ /a/ /t/ → cat. Play "what's missing?" by leaving out the beginning, middle, or ending sound. Build words with letter tiles, decode them, and use each one in a silly sentence. When this clicks, start reading short decodable books with simple CVC sentences. This is the magic moment — real reading.

6. Only then: long vowels, silent e, and beyond

Here's where patience pays off. Only after CVC words are mastered do we move on. Teach that vowels come in two kinds — long and short — and sort words by standing for long vowels and squatting for short ones (tap/tape, hop/hope). From there the roadmap continues:

  • Rhyming: words rhyme when they share the same middle and ending sound. First spot rhymes, then generate them.
  • Syllables: place a hand under the jaw — every time the jaw drops and touches the hand is one syllable. (It works every time!)
  • Silent e: the magic e that makes the vowel say its name — long and strong.
  • Digraphs: two letters, one sound — ch, wh, th, sh, ph.
  • Blends: bl in black, cr in crab — and double vowels like meat and boat.

Through all of it, keep practicing reading real sentences. The goal isn't just accuracy — it's confidence and fluency.

Common mistakes that slow this roadmap down

  • Rushing past CVC words because a child seems to "get it" after a day or two. Real mastery means reading them automatically, without sounding each one out from scratch, before moving to silent e and digraphs.
  • Skipping the sound-hearing stage (step 3) because it doesn't look like "real" reading yet. Children who struggle later with blending almost always need more practice here, not more practice with letters.
  • Introducing too many sight words too early, before decoding is solid. It creates a habit of guessing from memory instead of sounding out, which works for a while and then quietly stalls in 2nd or 3rd grade. (More in memorizing books vs. reading.)

Age-by-age expectations

Every child moves at their own pace, but roughly: ages 3–4 focus on letters, sounds, and hearing sounds in words — not decoding yet. Ages 4–5 move into consonants, vowels, and the first CVC words. Ages 5–6 build toward silent e, digraphs, and blends. By ages 6–7, the roadmap shifts to fluency and comprehension. If your child is well outside this range, it's not a reason to panic, but it's worth a closer look at which step needs more time.

Keep it joyful, and keep it short

Ten happy, focused minutes beat thirty tense ones. Be silly. Celebrate every win, even the shaky ones. A child who connects reading with warmth and success will keep reaching for it on their own — and that's the whole goal, more than any single skill on this roadmap.

Frequently asked questions

Do I have to teach every phonics skill in this exact order?
The broad sequence matters — letters and sounds before blending, CVC words before silent e and digraphs — because each step depends on the one before it. Within a stage, you have room to move at your child's pace. What matters most is not skipping ahead before a skill is truly solid.

My child knows their letters but can't blend sounds into words. What's wrong?
Usually nothing is wrong — blending is a distinct skill that takes practice separate from knowing letters. Go back to hearing sounds inside words (step 3) and practice with just two or three consonant sounds and one vowel before adding more. Most children need repeated, playful practice here before it clicks.

How long does it take to move through this whole roadmap?
It varies widely by child, but most children move through these stages over roughly one to two years, from around age 4 through kindergarten and into 1st grade. Slow, steady progress with real mastery at each stage beats rushing through and having to circle back later.
