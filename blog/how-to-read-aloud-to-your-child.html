How to Read Aloud to Your Child: Storytime That Builds Readers

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Reading aloud to your child is the single most powerful thing you can do for their future as a reader — and it's also the most fun. But there's a difference between simply reading the words and reading in a way that builds a reader. The good news? It's easy, and it mostly comes down to being a little more playful and a little more curious together.

Here's how I turn storytime into real reading skills — and how you can, too.

Start with the book itself

Before the story even begins, you can teach the parts of a book: the front cover, back cover, spine, the title, and the pages. Point out the direction of print — we read left to right, top to bottom. These tiny lessons take ten seconds and quietly teach your child how books work.

Name the story's building blocks

As you read, talk about the characters, the setting, and who the main character is. Point to the author and illustrator on the cover and explain what each one does. You can even introduce fiction (a made-up story) versus non-fiction (a true book that teaches us facts). Children absorb this vocabulary far earlier than most parents expect.

Make them part of the story

Comprehension grows when children participate, not just listen. A few of my favorite moves:

  • Fill in the blank. Pause on a familiar line and let them finish it.
  • Predict. "What do you think will happen next?"
  • Turn and talk. Ask a question, then have them tell you (or a sibling) the answer.
  • Add a motion. Pick a word that repeats and do an action every time it appears.
  • Picture read. Let your child "read" a page to you using the pictures as clues — this is real reading at the beginning.
  • Hunt for details. Find the funny thing on every page (Jan Brett famously hides a little hedgehog!).

Bring it to life

Use your voice. Fluctuate it — whisper for the sneaky part, boom for the giant. Try singing a whole book, or acting it out together. Use imagery: "Can you picture how cold the snow felt?" Pause frequently to ask questions. And most of all — be silly. The child who laughs during storytime is the child who comes back for more.

Keep coming back

Re-read favorites without guilt — repetition is how young children master language and story. Each reading, they notice something new and predict a little more. Familiar books build confidence; that confidence becomes a love of reading.

You're doing amazing

If you read with your child today — even one silly, joyful book — you gave them something lasting. Keep it up.
