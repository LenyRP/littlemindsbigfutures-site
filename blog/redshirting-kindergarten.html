Redshirting Kindergarten: Should You Hold Your Child Back a Year?

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Redshirting kindergarten means waiting an extra year before enrolling, usually because a child has a late summer birthday or parents feel they need more time to develop. There is no universal right answer. The best decision depends on your specific child — their social readiness, their skills, and your family's honest read of where they are.

Every July, parents across Williamson County face this question. Their child is age-eligible for kindergarten, but something feels off. Maybe they have a late August birthday. Maybe they're small for their age. Maybe they just seem young.

So should you hold them back?

As a certified early childhood specialist, I want to give you a framework for thinking through this — not a verdict. That decision belongs to you and your child.

What does redshirting kindergarten actually mean?

The term comes from college sports, where players sit out a year to develop before competing. In education, it means delaying kindergarten entry by one year, even though your child meets the age cutoff.

In Tennessee, the cutoff for kindergarten entry is September 30. A child born in August is technically eligible, but only a few weeks past the cutoff date. Many parents of late-summer children consider waiting.

Redshirting is more common than you might think. In areas like Franklin and Brentwood, where families tend to be highly invested in their children's school experience, it comes up often.

What are the possible benefits of waiting?

A year of additional development can help in real ways for some children:

  • More time for social-emotional growth. Kindergarten requires a child to manage emotions, follow group directions, and share an adult's attention with twenty other children. For some kids, twelve more months makes that much easier.
  • More physical readiness. Fine motor skills — pencil grip, cutting, managing a backpack — can mature considerably in a year.
  • More confidence walking in. A child who feels capable on day one tends to build on that. A child who struggles from the start can develop a story about themselves as someone who isn't good at school.

For some children, those benefits are real and meaningful.

What are the possible drawbacks?

Holding back is not a cost-free choice. A few things worth weighing:

  • An extra year at home or in pre-K costs time, money, and sometimes the social experience of being with a kindergarten peer group.
  • Children who are academically ready but held back sometimes get bored, and boredom in a young child is not harmless.
  • Being the oldest child in the class is not automatically an advantage — it depends on the child.
  • Some research suggests the advantage of redshirting fades by third or fourth grade for many children. Others find a lasting benefit. The data is genuinely mixed.

The honest answer is that it varies by child.

So how do I actually decide?

A few questions worth sitting with:

Is your child struggling with any specific skills, or do they just feel "young" to you? Feeling young is worth noting. Struggling with concrete skills is more actionable.

What does your child's preschool teacher say? A teacher who has watched your child in a group setting has information you don't have at home.

How does your child handle transitions and new places? A child who melts down every time the routine changes may benefit from more time. A child who walks into new situations with curiosity is probably ready.

What is your gut telling you? You know your child. If something genuinely feels off, that's worth honoring — as long as you're not making the decision from fear alone.

If you are unsure, the most useful thing you can do is get a clear picture of where your child is right now. A personalized readiness assessment with Leslie looks at all the areas that matter — literacy, math, social skills, fine motor — and gives you real information to take into that decision.

You can also download the free Kindergarten Readiness Checklist and walk through it yourself as a starting point.

What if I decide to wait?

A year is a gift when it's used well. Children who wait a year benefit most when that year includes intentional learning — reading practice, fine motor work, play-based math, and social time with other children.

Sitting at home in front of a screen is not the same as a year of intentional preparation. If you decide to wait, make the year count. Leslie's programs are built for exactly that — children who need focused, joyful preparation before the school door opens.

Frequently asked questions

Is redshirting more common for boys?

It is more common for boys than girls. Boys often develop socially and emotionally a bit later, and parents notice it. That said, the decision still comes down to the individual child, not gender alone.

Will my child's teacher think less of them for being older?

Good teachers do not think that way. They meet each child where they are. Age within the class is one factor among many.

Can I change my mind after the first few weeks of kindergarten?

In Tennessee, you can typically withdraw a child from kindergarten early in the year without it counting as retention. Talk to your school directly. But starting and stopping is harder on children than deciding before the year begins, so it's worth making the most informed choice you can now.
