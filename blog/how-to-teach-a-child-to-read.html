How to Teach a Child to Read: A Phonics-First Guide for Parents

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Teaching a child to read can feel mysterious — like there's a secret method everyone else knows. There isn't. Reading is built from a handful of skills that grow together, and when you nurture them side by side, most children thrive. Here's the approach I use with children every week, simplified for home.

Tune the ear to sounds, right alongside letters

As children learn their letters, they also need to hear the sounds inside words — this is called phonological awareness. Play rhyming games. Clap syllables in names. Ask "What sound does sun start with?" I teach sounds and letters together, because connecting the two is one of the strongest predictors of later reading success.

Teach letter sounds, not just letter names

Knowing the alphabet song isn't reading. A child needs to know that m says /mmm/. Teach a few sounds at a time, and use the most common, useful letters first (s, a, t, p, i, n) so they can build real words quickly.

Blend sounds into words

Once they know a handful of sounds, show them how to push the sounds together: /s/ /a/ /t/ → sat. This is the magic moment — decoding. Start with short, three-sound words and celebrate every success. This is phonics, and it's why phonics-first instruction produces lasting results instead of guesswork.

Add a few "heart words"

Some common words (the, was, said) don't follow the rules and have to be learned by heart. Keep these to a handful at a time so decoding stays the main event.

Read together — a lot

Practice with simple books that match the sounds they know, so they experience success, not frustration. Re-read favorites for fluency. And keep reading aloud to them well above their level — that's where vocabulary and a love of story grow.

Keep it joyful, keep it short

Ten focused, happy minutes beat thirty tense ones. Stop while they still want more. A child who associates reading with warmth and success will keep going on their own — and that's the whole goal.
