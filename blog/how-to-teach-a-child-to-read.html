How to Teach a Child to Read: A Phonics-First Guide for Parents

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Teach a child to read in this order: hear sounds inside words, learn letter sounds (not just letter names), blend sounds into simple words, add a few sight words that don't follow the rules, then read real books together often. Keep sessions short — ten focused minutes beats thirty tense ones.

Teaching a child to read can feel mysterious — like there's a secret method everyone else knows and you missed the memo. There isn't a secret. Reading is built from a handful of skills that grow together, and when you nurture them side by side, in the right order, most children thrive. Here's the approach I use with children every week in Williamson County, simplified so you can do it at your own kitchen table.

One thing I tell every parent on our first call: you don't need a teaching degree to do this well. You need a clear order to follow and the patience to stay in it for a few minutes a day. That's genuinely most of the job.

Tune the ear to sounds, right alongside letters

As children learn their letters, they also need to hear the sounds inside words — this is called phonological awareness, and it's the quiet foundation almost everything else sits on. Play rhyming games in the car. Clap syllables in family members' names. Ask "What sound does sun start with?" while you're making lunch. I teach sounds and letters together, because connecting the two is one of the strongest predictors of later reading success — stronger, in fact, than knowing the alphabet by itself.

A simple daily habit: pick two words at breakfast and ask which sound they start with, or whether they rhyme. It takes thirty seconds and it works.

Teach letter sounds, not just letter names

Knowing the alphabet song isn't reading. A child can sing "L-M-N-O-P" perfectly and still have no idea what sound the letter m makes. They need to know that m says /mmm/, not "em." Teach a few sounds at a time, and use the most common, useful letters first — s, a, t, p, i, n — because those six letters alone let a child build dozens of real words almost immediately. Nothing builds momentum like early wins.

Common mistake to avoid: teaching all 26 letters before starting any blending. Children lose interest long before letter 26. Start blending as soon as they know a handful of sounds.

Blend sounds into words

Once they know a handful of sounds, show them how to push the sounds together: /s/ /a/ /t/ → sat. This is the magic moment — decoding. Start with short, three-sound words (called CVC words — consonant, vowel, consonant) and celebrate every success, even the shaky ones. This is phonics, and it's why phonics-first instruction produces lasting, transferable results instead of guesswork that falls apart on new words. (If you want the exact order I move through phonics skills after this stage, I've written it all out in what order to teach phonics.)

A good practice tool here is letter tiles or magnetic letters — physically sliding sounds together helps many children "get it" faster than looking at a printed word alone. If you want to understand exactly what these words are and why they matter so much at this stage, see CVC words explained.

Add a few "heart words"

Some common words (the, was, said) don't follow the rules and have to be learned by heart rather than sounded out. Keep these to a small handful at a time so decoding stays the main event, not memorization. Flash cards are fine for these specific words — just don't let the whole reading program turn into flash cards. That's a trap I see often, and it's worth understanding why it backfires: why phonics beats memorizing.

Read together — a lot, and in two different ways

There are actually two kinds of reading time, and both matter. First, practice reading with simple, decodable books that match the sounds your child already knows, so they experience success, not frustration. Re-read favorites for fluency — rereading is not "cheating," it's how automaticity is built. Second, keep reading aloud to your child well above their own reading level. That's where vocabulary, sentence structure, and a genuine love of story grow, long before your child could read those books alone. For a deeper look at building that second kind of habit, see how to read aloud to your child.

Age-by-age notes

Every child is different, but here's a rough map of what tends to happen when: at ages 3–4, focus almost entirely on rhyming, letter sounds, and being read to — not decoding yet. At ages 4–5, most children are ready to start blending simple CVC words. At ages 5–6, decoding should be picking up speed, moving into digraphs, blends, and silent e. By ages 6–7, the goal shifts toward fluency and comprehension — reading with expression and actually understanding what was read, not just decoding accurately. If your child is outside this rough timeline in either direction, that's not automatically a problem, but it's worth a closer look.

Common mistakes parents make with good intentions

  • Correcting every single mistake immediately. Let your child try to self-correct first. Constant interruption erodes confidence fast.
  • Moving to harder books too soon because a child seems bored. Boredom at an easy level is often a sign to add more fluency practice, not to skip ahead.
  • Turning practice into a test. Quizzing feels like pressure to a 5-year-old. Playful practice teaches the same skill without the anxiety.

Keep it joyful, keep it short

Ten focused, happy minutes beat thirty tense ones, every single time. Stop while your child still wants more — that's what makes them ask for it again tomorrow. A child who associates reading with warmth and success will keep going on their own, and that's the whole goal, far more than any single skill on this list.

Frequently asked questions

What age should I start teaching my child to read?
You can start the groundwork as early as age 3 with rhyming, letter sounds, and read-aloud time. Most children are ready to begin blending sounds into words between ages 4 and 6, though every child moves at their own pace — the skills matter more than the calendar.

How long should reading practice take each day?
Ten to fifteen focused minutes is plenty for a young child. Short and joyful beats long and tense every time. If your child is engaged and wants to keep going, follow their lead — but never push past the point where it stops feeling good.

What if my child already knows some sight words but can't sound out new ones?
That's a common sign the phonics foundation needs more attention. Memorizing whole words works for a while, but it breaks down once books get harder. Go back to letter sounds and blending — teaching your child to decode, not just recognize, is what builds a reader who can tackle any word.
