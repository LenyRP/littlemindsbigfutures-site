How to Prepare Your Child for the First Day of Kindergarten

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Preparing for the first day of kindergarten means addressing two things: your child's emotional readiness and their academic foundation. Start with the emotional piece — fear of the unknown is usually the bigger hurdle. Then layer in simple skill practice so they walk in feeling capable.

The first day of kindergarten is close. You can feel it — the mix of excitement and nerves, the new backpack hanging by the door, the questions your child keeps asking about what school will be like.

As a certified early childhood specialist, I've helped many families in Williamson County get ready for this day. And I'll tell you what I always tell parents: the children who do best on day one are not always the ones with the most academic preparation. They're the ones who feel safe.

Let's build that feeling together.

How do I help my child feel emotionally ready?

Children fear what they can't picture. The first step in preparing for the first day of kindergarten is making the unknown familiar.

Visit the school before opening day. Many schools offer a "meet the teacher" night or an orientation visit. Go. Walk the halls. Find the bathroom. Let your child sit in a real kindergarten chair. That ten-minute walk removes weeks of imagined fear.

Talk about the day in concrete pictures. "You'll hang your backpack on a hook with your name on it. Then you'll sit on a rug while the teacher reads a story." Specific details are more comforting than "it will be great."

Practice the goodbye. If your child has never been away from you in a structured setting, the drop-off moment can be hard. Practice brief, cheerful goodbyes at preschool, a playdate, or a church class. Keep them short. A long, worried goodbye tells your child something is wrong.

Name the feelings. "It's okay to feel nervous. New things can feel like that. And you will figure it out, because you always do." That kind of confidence from you lands in them.

What academic skills help most on the first day?

You don't need a summer of worksheets. A few specific skills make the first weeks much smoother.

Know their name in print. A child who can recognize and write their first name navigates the kindergarten classroom more confidently. Their name is everywhere — on cubbies, on folders, on name tags.

Follow two-step directions. Kindergarten teachers give directions in sequence. "Take out your crayon box and put it on your desk." Practice this at home. Give two-step instructions during everyday routines.

Hold a pencil correctly. Fine motor habits form early and are hard to undo. A few minutes a day with crayons, playdough, or lacing beads builds the hand strength that makes writing easier. Visit Leslie's programs page if your child needs targeted fine motor support.

Know how to handle their own things. Opening a lunchbox, putting on a jacket, managing a zipper. These small independence skills take pressure off both the teacher and your child.

Read together every night. You don't have to teach them to read before kindergarten. But a child who has been read to thousands of times knows how books work, loves stories, and is ready to learn. That is a real head start.

What should I do in the last two weeks before school?

The two weeks before school starts are worth using intentionally.

  1. Adjust bedtime. Move it earlier, gradually. A tired child on the first day of kindergarten has a much harder time. Start now, not the night before.
  1. Do a practice run. Drive or walk the route to school. Time it. Park and look around. Familiarity builds calm.
  1. Practice the morning routine. Wake up at school time. Get dressed, eat breakfast, and pack the backpack. Do this a few days in a row so the morning of day one feels normal.
  1. Let your child pick one thing. A special pencil, a keychain on the backpack, a photo tucked inside. That small piece of home goes with them.
  1. Download the free Kindergarten Readiness Checklist if you haven't already. Walk through it now and address any gaps you find.

What if my child is very anxious?

Some anxiety is completely healthy. It means your child understands that something new is coming. That's good.

If the anxiety is intense — tears for weeks, refusal to talk about school, physical complaints in the morning — that's worth paying attention to. A conversation with Leslie can help you sort out whether your child needs more readiness work, more emotional coaching, or just reassurance from someone who has walked many families through this exact moment.

Frequently asked questions

Should I tell my child that kindergarten will be fun?

You can, but pair it with honesty. "It might feel weird at first. That's okay. After a few days, you'll start to know where things are and who your friends are." False certainty can backfire when day one is harder than expected.

My child went to preschool. Are they automatically ready?

Preschool is a great foundation, but not every preschool covers the same skills. A child who attended preschool may still have gaps in letter recognition or pencil grip. The checklist will help you see.

What if they cry at drop-off?

Many children do on the first day, and most stop within minutes of the parent leaving. Trust the teacher. Keep your own goodbye brief and confident. Check in with the teacher at pickup — not during the day — and you'll almost always hear that everything was fine.
