Summer Slide: How to Prevent Learning Loss Before School Starts

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Every fall, teachers spend the first weeks of school re-teaching what students forgot over the summer. It even has a name: the "summer slide." On average, children can lose a month or more of reading progress between June and August — and for early readers, those months matter enormously.

Here's the encouraging part: it's completely preventable, and it doesn't take a rigid summer of worksheets. A little, consistently, goes a long way.

Why summer hits early readers hardest

Reading is a skill, like a muscle — it weakens without use. A child who just learned to decode in kindergarten hasn't yet made those skills automatic. Three months of no practice can undo a year of careful growth, and they walk into first grade behind where they ended kindergarten.

5 simple ways to keep skills sharp

1. Read together every day — even 15 minutes. It's the single highest-impact thing you can do. Let them choose the books. Re-reading favorites counts; repetition builds fluency.

2. Make it part of the routine, not a battle. Same time each day — after lunch, before bed. Consistency beats intensity every time.

3. Bring reading into real life. Recipes, road signs, menus, grocery lists. Show them that reading is everywhere and useful, not just a school thing.

4. Play with sounds and numbers. Rhyming games in the car, counting steps, "I spy something that starts with /b/." Learning disguised as play is still learning.

5. Keep a little structure. For children with gaps, summer is actually the best time for focused tutoring — no homework competing for attention, and steady progress without pressure.

What a summer week actually looks like

If a "summer learning plan" sounds heavy, here's the whole thing in practice. Monday: a library trip where your child picks the books — any books, even the same dinosaur one for the fourth week running. Tuesday through Friday: fifteen minutes of reading together after lunch, with your child "reading" the pictures on one page while you read the words on the rest. In the car: rhyming games, or "I spy something that starts with /b/." At dinner: everyone shares one word they heard today that starts with the letter of the week. That's the entire plan. No desk, no tears, no fighting — and by mid-August it quietly adds up to more than twenty hours of practice.

One tip that makes the whole thing stick: tie reading to something that already happens every day. After lunch, before rest time, right after bath. When it's attached to a routine, you never have to decide whether today is a reading day.

Don't forget the math

Reading gets all the summer-slide attention, but early math skills fade the very same way. The fix is just as painless: count the strawberries while you wash them, compare who has more watermelon, measure the tomato plant every Friday and mark it on the fence. I've collected my favorite no-prep ideas in everyday math activities, and for the moments when you're waiting for a table or sitting in a pickup line, these 5-minute learning games cover letters and numbers in the time you'd otherwise spend handing over a phone.

If your child starts kindergarten in August

For rising kindergartners, summer isn't about preventing loss — it's about laying a foundation. Practice the things teachers hope to see in week one: recognizing their written name, counting a handful of objects by touching each one, sitting through a whole story, opening their own lunchbox. The routine itself deserves rehearsal too. Summer is an opportunity, not just a risk

The same months that cause the slide can be used to get ahead. A child who keeps reading over the summer doesn't just hold their place — they walk into the new grade with momentum and confidence. Frequently asked questions

How much time a day does it take to prevent the summer slide?
For most children, 15 to 20 minutes of reading together plus a few playful minutes of counting or letter games is plenty. Consistency matters far more than length — a short session every day does more than a long one every Saturday.

Should we use workbooks over the summer?
You don't have to. Real books, car games, and everyday practice like reading menus and counting change do the same job with far less resistance. If your child genuinely enjoys workbook pages, a few here and there are fine — just stop before it starts to feel like a chore, because protecting their attitude toward learning matters more than any single page.

Does the summer slide affect a child who hasn't started school yet?
In a different way, yes. A child entering kindergarten in August isn't losing school skills, but summer is prime time to build the routines and pre-reading skills that make those first weeks easier. Think of it as a runway rather than a rescue.

When is summer tutoring worth it?
If your child ended the school year behind, avoids reading, or lost confidence, summer is honestly the best season for tutoring. There's no homework competing for energy, so a few focused sessions each week can close a gap quietly before the new grade begins.
