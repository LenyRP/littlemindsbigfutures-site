Kindergarten Readiness Checklist: A Parent's 5-Minute Guide

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: A kindergarten readiness checklist covers five areas — social-emotional skills, early literacy, early math, physical skills, and self-care. Walk through it now to see where your child is strong and where a little extra support could help. Download the free 25-point version at the link below.

Is your child ready for kindergarten? That question keeps a lot of parents up at night. As a certified early childhood specialist serving families across Williamson County, I hear it constantly. The good news is that readiness is not a pass-or-fail test. It's a picture of where your child is right now — and a kindergarten readiness checklist is the clearest way to see that picture.

Give yourself five minutes. Let's walk through it together.

What does a kindergarten readiness checklist actually cover?

Most checklists look at five areas. Think of them as five windows into your child's development.

1. Social and emotional skills

Kindergarten is a social place. Teachers need children who can:

  • Separate from a parent without extended crying
  • Follow simple two-step directions ("Put your backpack away, then sit on the rug")
  • Take turns and share, at least some of the time
  • Handle frustration without falling apart every time

Your child does not need to be perfect here. They need to be working on it.

2. Early literacy skills

Reading instruction starts in kindergarten, but these foundations come first:

  • Recognizing and writing their own first name
  • Knowing that print goes left to right
  • Identifying some letters, especially the ones in their name
  • Understanding that letters stand for sounds

If your child loves books and asks about words, that curiosity is already working in their favor. If they're not there yet, that's exactly what programs like Jumpstart and Leaping are built for.

3. Early math skills

Kindergarten math is concrete and hands-on. Children benefit from being able to:

  • Count objects reliably to ten or beyond
  • Recognize and name basic shapes
  • Understand simple concepts like "more" and "less"
  • Sort objects by color, size, or shape

4. Physical and fine-motor skills

These matter more than parents often expect.

  • Holding a crayon or pencil with a reasonable grip
  • Cutting with child scissors
  • Turning pages in a book one at a time
  • Managing zippers, buttons, or snaps on their own clothing

Fine motor work is quiet practice — puzzles, playdough, lacing beads — and it pays off when your child sits down to write.

5. Self-care and independence

Teachers genuinely appreciate children who can handle the basics on their own:

  • Using the bathroom independently
  • Opening a lunchbox or snack bag
  • Following the classroom routine after a week or two

How do I use the checklist?

Sit down with it when your child is well-rested and in a good mood. Read each item. Note which ones your child does consistently, which ones they're working on, and which ones haven't started yet. The "working on it" column is not a red flag. It's your action list.

Once you know the gaps, you can address them deliberately. A few minutes of focused practice each day goes a long way between now and the first day of school.

If you'd like a ready-to-use version, grab the free 25-point Kindergarten Readiness Checklist here. It covers all five areas and includes simple notes on what to look for.

What if my child is missing several areas?

First, take a breath. Many children entering kindergarten in Franklin, Brentwood, and Nolensville are still working on one or two areas. That is completely normal.

What matters is what you do next. A personalized assessment with Leslie can identify exactly where your child needs support and build a focused plan before school starts. That kind of targeted help in the weeks before August makes a real difference.

When should I start working through the checklist?

July is actually a good time — early enough to act on what you find, late enough that the skills are relevant. If you are reading this in spring, even better. More runway means more time to build at a gentle pace.

Whatever time of year it is, starting now is better than waiting. Children build confidence one small skill at a time. God gave every little mind a way to learn; sometimes they just need someone to find it with them.

Frequently asked questions

Does my child have to check every box before starting kindergarten?

No. Kindergarten teachers expect a range of readiness. The checklist is a guide, not a gate. Knowing which skills are still developing helps you and Leslie plan a focused, calm summer or fall.

My child checks most boxes but struggles with letters. Is that a problem?

Not necessarily. Letter recognition varies widely at age four and five. If you notice difficulty, a quick conversation with Leslie can tell you whether it's a timing issue or something worth addressing before school starts.

Is the checklist the same for every school?

The core skills are very similar across Williamson County schools, though each school may have its own intake process. The five areas above align with what most kindergarten teachers look for.
