7 Signs Your Child Is Struggling to Read (and How to Help)

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Watch for a child who avoids reading, guesses at words instead of sounding them out, can't hear sounds inside words, reads slowly and choppily, can't retell what they read, reverses letters past 1st grade, or is losing confidence. One sign on an off day is nothing. A cluster of them, week after week, is your cue to look closer.

Almost every parent I meet in Williamson County asks me some version of the same question: "Is this normal, or is my child actually behind?" It's one of the hardest things to judge from the inside — especially with reading, where children are wonderfully good at hiding what they can't yet do. A child can look busy with a book for years without actually reading a word of it.

The good news: reading struggles are among the most fixable challenges in early education, when they're caught early. Here are seven signs worth paying attention to, what each one usually means, and what to do about it.

1. They avoid reading — or get "tired" the moment a book appears

Sudden fatigue, tummy aches, or distraction at reading time is rarely about energy. It's usually avoidance of something that feels hard. Children protect themselves from failure by opting out, and it can look like anything from a meltdown to a sudden, urgent need for a snack the second a book comes out. If reading time has turned into a daily negotiation, that's worth paying attention to — and if the negotiations end in tears more often than not, helping a frustrated learner walks through what to do in those moments.

2. They guess at words instead of sounding them out

If your child looks at the first letter and guesses ("h…horse?" for house), or looks at the picture instead of the word, they may be leaning on memory and pictures rather than decoding. Strong readers are built on phonics — connecting letters to sounds — not guessing. This strategy can actually look like reading for a while, especially with simple picture books, which is exactly why it's easy to miss. It tends to fall apart once the books get harder and the pictures stop giving away the answer.

3. They can't hear the sounds inside words

Ask: "What sound does cat start with?" or "Can you rhyme hat?" A child who struggles here often has a gap in phonological awareness — a foundational skill that predicts reading success more than almost anything else, even more than knowing the alphabet. The good news is this is also one of the fastest gaps to close with playful, targeted practice.

4. Reading is slow, choppy, and word-by-word

Fluency is the bridge between decoding and understanding. If every word is a struggle, there's no mental energy left to comprehend the story — which makes reading feel pointless and exhausting to a child, even when they technically "can" sound out most of the words. This is a common stage that many children pass through briefly on the way to real fluency; it becomes a concern when it persists for months without improvement. (More on building this skill in building reading fluency.)

5. They read it, but can't tell you what happened

If your child decodes the words but can't answer "What was the story about?", comprehension is the gap. Sometimes it's vocabulary, sometimes it's that decoding is taking all their focus, leaving nothing left over for actually understanding the sentence. Ask your child to retell a simple story in their own words after reading it — if that's consistently hard, it's a sign worth noting.

6. They reverse letters well past kindergarten

Flipping b and d, or writing letters backward, is completely normal at 4 and 5 — nearly every young child does this while their brain is still sorting out left and right. Past first grade, persistent reversals paired with other signs on this list are worth a closer look. On their own, occasional reversals in a 6- or 7-year-old are usually nothing to worry about.

7. Their confidence is sinking

"I'm just bad at reading." When a child starts to believe that, the academic gap becomes an emotional one — and that's the one that follows them longest, sometimes well beyond the reading gap itself. Protecting a child's belief in themselves is half the work, and it often has to happen alongside the academic instruction, not after it. In quieter children this can be easy to miss entirely — building confidence in shy learners looks at how it shows up when a child withdraws instead of complains.

How these signs show up differently by age

A preschooler (age 3–4) showing these signs mostly needs more rhyming games, letter-sound play, and read-aloud time — there's rarely cause for concern this early, just a nudge to build the groundwork. A kindergartner or 1st grader showing several of these signs together is the age range where a closer look pays off most, since the foundation is actively being built right now. By 2nd or 3rd grade, these signs usually point to a specific missing piece from earlier stages rather than a brand-new problem — which is genuinely good news, because it means we know exactly what to go back and fix. (If this is where your child is, is it too late for my child to learn to read answers the question most parents ask next.)

What to do next

One or two of these on an off day? Probably nothing. But a cluster of them, week after week, is your cue to act — and the earlier the better. Reading gaps don't close on their own; they widen quietly until they show up as a confidence problem, which is harder and slower to repair than the reading skill itself.

Frequently asked questions

How many of these signs mean my child needs help?
One or two on an off day isn't usually cause for concern. But a cluster of these signs showing up consistently, week after week, is your cue to look closer. Trust the pattern more than any single moment.

Are reversing letters like b and d always a sign of a reading problem?
No. Letter reversals are completely normal in preschool and kindergarten, up through about age 6 or 7. It's only a concern when it persists well past 1st grade and shows up alongside other signs on this list, like trouble decoding or avoiding reading altogether.

What should I do first if I notice several of these signs?
Start by getting a clear picture of where your child actually stands — our free Kindergarten Readiness Checklist is a five-minute way to do that at home. From there, a short conversation with a certified early childhood specialist can tell you whether it's a quick fix or needs ongoing support.
