7 Signs Your Child Is Struggling to Read (and How to Help)

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Almost every parent I meet in Williamson County asks me some version of the same question: "Is this normal, or is my child actually behind?" It's one of the hardest things to judge from the inside — especially with reading, where children are wonderfully good at hiding what they can't yet do.

The good news: reading struggles are among the most fixable challenges in early education, when they're caught early. Here are seven signs worth paying attention to, and what each one usually means.

1. They avoid reading — or get "tired" the moment a book appears

Sudden fatigue, tummy aches, or distraction at reading time is rarely about energy. It's usually avoidance of something that feels hard. Children protect themselves from failure by opting out.

2. They guess at words instead of sounding them out

If your child looks at the first letter and guesses ("h…horse?" for house), they may be leaning on memory and pictures rather than decoding. Strong readers are built on phonics — connecting letters to sounds — not guessing.

3. They can't hear the sounds inside words

Ask: "What sound does cat start with?" or "Can you rhyme hat?" A child who struggles here often has a gap in phonological awareness — a foundational skill that predicts reading success more than almost anything else.

4. Reading is slow, choppy, and word-by-word

Fluency is the bridge between decoding and understanding. If every word is a struggle, there's no mental energy left to comprehend the story — which makes reading feel pointless to a child.

5. They read it, but can't tell you what happened

If your child decodes the words but can't answer "What was the story about?", comprehension is the gap. Sometimes it's vocabulary, sometimes it's that decoding is taking all their focus.

6. They reverse letters well past kindergarten

Flipping b and d is normal at 4 and 5. Past first grade, persistent reversals paired with other signs on this list are worth a closer look.

7. Their confidence is sinking

"I'm just bad at reading." When a child starts to believe that, the academic gap becomes an emotional one — and that's the one that follows them. Protecting a child's belief in themselves is half the work.

What to do next

One or two of these on an off day? Probably nothing. But a cluster of them, week after week, is your cue to act — and the earlier the better. Reading gaps don't close on their own; they widen quietly until they show up as a confidence problem.
