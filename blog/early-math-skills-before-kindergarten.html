Early Math Skills: What to Teach Before Kindergarten (and the Order That Works)

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Math, for little ones, isn't worksheets. It's blocks, snacks, sidewalk chalk, and counting steps to the car. At this age, math lives in your child's hands — and the more they touch, sort, and move real objects, the more the numbers actually mean something. Here's the order I build early math skills in, written so you can do it at home with things you already own.

Keep this in mind throughout: always use visuals and manipulatives. Buttons, blocks, cereal, toy cars — anything a child can hold makes an abstract idea concrete. And like all things this age, the magic is repetition. A little, often, beats a lot, once.

1. Counting out loud — every single day

Start by counting to 20 every day for about a month, then stretch to 30 and beyond. Once they're comfortable, introduce counting by 10s, and then by 5s. Count stairs, count goldfish crackers, count how many jumps to the mailbox. Daily counting wires the number sequence into memory before any "math" is ever required.

2. Putting numbers in order

Next, practice ordering numbers 1–10, then 1–20. Number cards you can shuffle and line up are perfect — mix them, then put them back in order together. This is where counting turns into understanding that numbers have a fixed sequence and that each one is "one more" than the last.

3. Sorting, classifying, and attributes

Before formal math, children need to notice how things are alike and different. Sort by color and shape. Classify a pile of toys into groups. Talk about attributes — which is heavier, lighter, softer, longer, shorter — then sort by those, too. This quiet skill is the foundation of comparing, measuring, and patterns later on.

4. Shapes and position words

Go well beyond circle and square. Children this age can learn circle, triangle, oval, octagon, rectangle, square, trapezoid, star, crescent, semi-circle, and rhombus — and love showing off the fancy names. Then have fun creating new shapes from old ones (two triangles make a square!). Layer in position words as you play: over, under, above, below, right, left. "Put the bear under the chair" is real math.

5. Writing and comparing numbers 1–20

Now bring numbers to paper: writing numerals 1–20, and comparing them — which is bigger, which is smaller. Introduce how to tally, too. Counting on fingers and objects should still be welcome; we're connecting the spoken number, the written numeral, and the actual quantity.

6. The first taste of adding and subtracting

Only once counting and quantity feel solid do we gently introduce addition and subtraction within 10 — and always with objects in hand. "You have 3 grapes, here are 2 more, how many now?" Let them physically push the groups together and count. Never rush to symbols; the hands lead, the numbers follow.

7. Measuring and symmetry

Round things out with measuring objects (with blocks, paper clips, or a ruler) and a first look at symmetry — folding a paper heart, finding the matching halves of a butterfly. These bring math into the real, visual world and keep it feeling like discovery rather than drill.

A gentle reminder

Your child does not need to master all of this before kindergarten — this is the path, not a checklist for tomorrow. Stay playful, keep it hands-on, and celebrate the small wins.
