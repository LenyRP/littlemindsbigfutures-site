Decodable Books vs. Leveled Readers: Which Should Your Child Use?

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Decodable books are designed for children who are still learning phonics — every word is built from sounds they've already studied. Leveled readers match a child's overall reading ability but may include words that can't be sounded out yet. For beginning readers, decodable books build the strongest foundation.

If you've been researching books for your early reader, you've probably run into both terms — decodable books and leveled readers — and wondered what the actual difference is. It's a question families in Franklin and across Williamson County ask me regularly. As a certified early childhood specialist, I want to give you a clear picture so you can choose well.

The answer matters because the right type of book at the right stage can make a big difference in how quickly and confidently your child learns to read.

What are decodable books?

Decodable books are written to match specific phonics patterns. If a child has learned short vowels and consonant blends, a decodable book written for that stage will use only words that follow those rules — plus a small number of common sight words.

The point is that a child can actually sound out almost everything on the page. They're not guessing. They're not relying on pictures. They're practicing the exact skill that reading requires: converting letters into sounds and blending them into words.

This feels controlled and simple, and that's the idea. Success at the right level builds confidence and reinforces that the phonics code actually works.

What are leveled readers?

Leveled readers — books labeled A through Z or using a numbered level system — are matched to a child's general reading ability based on vocabulary, sentence length, and concept load. They're widely used in schools and at-home programs.

The challenge with leveled readers for beginning decoders is that many books at the early levels still contain words that can't be sounded out. A child who doesn't yet know all their phonics patterns has to fill in those gaps somehow, usually through picture clues, guessing from context, or memorizing common words.

For a child who's still learning to decode, that guessing habit can get in the way. If every time a hard word appears, a child looks at the picture rather than the letters, they're not building the skill that reading actually requires.

Leveled readers are more appropriate once phonics is solid — once a child can decode fluently and is ready to work on comprehension, vocabulary, and more complex text.

When should my child use decodable books?

Use decodable books when your child is:

  • Still learning letter sounds and phonics patterns
  • Guessing at words based on pictures rather than sounding them out
  • Reading familiar books from memory but struggling with new text (sound familiar? Here's more on that pattern)
  • In kindergarten or early first grade and just beginning to decode

The decodable stage doesn't last forever. As your child learns more phonics patterns, the books available to them grow quickly. Think of decodables as the scaffolding that comes down once the building is standing.

When should my child use leveled readers?

Leveled readers become more useful when your child:

  • Can sound out most words without needing pictures for clues
  • Reads new text (not just memorized books) with reasonable accuracy
  • Is ready to work on fluency and comprehension rather than decoding

If your child's teacher is sending home leveled readers and your child is still early in their phonics journey, it's worth asking the teacher which phonics patterns have been covered. Then look for books that stick close to those patterns.

Can they use both?

Yes. Many families use decodable books for explicit phonics practice and read picture books or leveled readers alongside for fun and comprehension. The key is making sure decodable books are the practice reading — the place where decoding skills get built — rather than treating all reading as the same kind of activity.

If you're working on reading fluency, slightly easier decodable texts are ideal for repeated reading practice. They keep the focus on smoothness rather than decoding work.

Frequently asked questions

My child's school only uses leveled readers. Should I be worried?
Not necessarily. Many schools are moving toward more phonics-aligned instruction, and some leveled readers are better than others. The question to ask is whether your child's reading program includes explicit, systematic phonics instruction. If it does, leveled readers are a smaller concern. If it doesn't, supplementing with decodables at home can help.

Are there brands of decodable books you'd recommend?
There are several good options — Bob Books sets, Flyleaf Publishing, Dandelion Readers, and Primary Phonics. What to choose depends on where your child is in their phonics sequence. A specialist can match books to what your child has already learned.

My child finds decodable books boring. What do I do?
Decodable books are designed for practice, so they're sometimes short on plot. Keep them paired with picture books and read-alouds for joy. The practice and the enjoyment can coexist — they just come from different books.
