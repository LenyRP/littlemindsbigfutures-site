How Many Books Should I Read to My Child Each Day?

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Aim for at least one book per day with your child — but more is always welcome. What matters most is that you read together every single day, even if it's just one short book at bedtime. Consistency beats quantity.

How many books should you read to your child each day? It's one of the most common questions I hear from parents, and the good news is that the answer is simple. As a certified early childhood specialist, my guidance is this: read at least one book every day, and if you can fit in two or three, even better. The key word is every. A daily read-aloud habit — even a short one — does far more than occasional longer sessions.

Why does daily reading matter so much?

Reading aloud to your child every day builds language and literacy in ways that almost nothing else can match at this age. It grows vocabulary. It builds listening comprehension. It introduces story structure, which helps children understand what they read long before they decode on their own. It models fluent reading — the rhythm, the expression, the pausing at a period.

Children who are read to daily from early childhood arrive at school with much larger vocabularies than peers who weren't. Vocabulary is one of the strongest predictors of reading comprehension in the school years. That daily habit, small as it seems, compounds over years in ways that matter enormously.

And beyond the skills: children remember being read to. It is one of the warmest, most ordinary gifts you can give them.

How many books is the right number?

One book a day is the floor, not the ceiling. Here's a simple way to think about it by age:

Ages 3–4: Two to four short books a day is a great target. Board books and picture books at this age are short, so this might mean only 15–20 minutes of reading spread across the day. Morning, naptime, and bedtime are natural slots.

Ages 5–6: One to three books a day still works well. At this age, picture books and early readers are a little longer, so one good read-aloud session of 20–30 minutes covers the goal. Children at this age may also be starting to read simple books themselves — that counts too, and should be celebrated.

Ages 7–8: One longer book shared together each day is plenty, especially if your child is also reading independently. Reading one chapter of a chapter book together at bedtime builds a different kind of love of reading — the ongoing story that pulls them back tomorrow.

If you want to go deeper on the how — pace, expression, comprehension questions — see how to read aloud to your child for the full guide.

What if we miss a day?

You will. Life happens, especially in the summer. A missed day is not a setback. What you want to protect is the habit — the expectation in your household that reading together is just what you do. When a day gets missed, pick up the next day without guilt or fanfare.

The habit builds in the long run, not in any single session.

What if my child won't sit still for a whole book?

This is very normal, especially for children under four. A few things that help:

  • Start shorter. Even three pages of a picture book together is a reading session if it's warm and connected.
  • Let them hold the book and turn the pages. Physical involvement helps.
  • Read the same book every night for a week. Repetition is not boredom for young children — it's mastery, and they often catch more each time.
  • Let them "read" to you. A child narrating a picture book from memory is building real literacy skills.

As attention develops, the sessions naturally grow longer. Don't rush it.

Does it count if we listen to an audiobook?

Audiobooks have real value — vocabulary, story exposure, listening skills. They work well in the car or during rest time. But they don't replace the warm togetherness of reading side by side. Sitting with a book, looking at the illustrations, pausing to talk about what just happened — that interaction is part of what makes read-alouds so powerful for young children.

Use both if you can. Audiobooks during errands, read-alouds at bedtime.

What about summer reading programs?

Summer reading programs at local libraries — including those in Franklin and Brentwood — are a wonderful addition to a daily reading habit. They add motivation, especially for children who are competitive or like earning rewards. They don't replace the daily read-aloud at home, but they layer in extra books and a sense of community around reading. If your child's library has one running, it's worth joining.

For children who are struggling with reading or who are working to get ready for kindergarten, summer is also a great time to add some intentional, targeted support. A personalized assessment can tell you exactly where your child is and what would help them most before September. And our free Kindergarten Readiness Checklist is a good place to start if you want a quick picture of where things stand.

Frequently asked questions

Does it count when my child reads to me?
Absolutely. A child reading aloud to you — even if they're mostly memorizing a familiar book — is practicing real literacy behaviors. Celebrate it, and keep going with your own read-alouds too.

We read a lot on weekends but barely during the week. Is that okay?
Weekend reading is wonderful, and definitely better than nothing. But daily consistency — even five minutes on a busy Tuesday — builds the habit more reliably. Try attaching reading to something you already do every day, like breakfast or bedtime.

My older child says they're too old to be read to. What do I do?
Chapter books read aloud together can hook even reluctant older readers — choose something with adventure, humor, or mystery. Many children who "outgrow" picture books fall back in love with reading when a great chapter book is read to them out loud, one chapter at a time.
