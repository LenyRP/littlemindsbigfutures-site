Why a Love of Learning Matters More Than Perfect Grades

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: Raising a child who loves learning matters more than perfect grades because curiosity keeps a child growing long after a single test is forgotten. Grades capture one moment. A love of learning shapes all the moments after it.

If your child brings home a sticker chart full of gold stars, that's wonderful. But here's what I've seen in years of working with little ones across Williamson County. The children who thrive aren't always the ones with the neatest worksheets. They're the ones who want to know what happens next in the story. They raise their hand because they're curious. Raising a child who loves learning is the quiet advantage that outlasts any report card.

As a certified early childhood specialist, I get to watch that curiosity take hold. And I promise you, it's worth protecting.

Why do grades matter less than we think for young kids?

For children ages 3–8, "grades" barely exist yet. A score is a snapshot. It tells you how a child did on one task, on one morning, in one mood. It can't tell you whether your child will still reach for books at age twelve, or ask "but why?" at the dinner table for years to come.

A love of learning works differently. It builds on itself. A curious child:

  1. Tries longer before giving up, because figuring it out is the fun part.
  2. Recovers from mistakes faster, because a mistake is just something interesting to fix.
  3. Keeps learning on their own time — reading the cereal box, counting the stairs at bedtime.

That third one is where the real growth lives. You can't sit beside your child every minute. A curious child keeps going when you're not looking.

What does a love of learning actually look like?

It's smaller and simpler than you might expect:

  • Asking question after question, even the exhausting ones.
  • Wanting the same book again, and noticing something new each time.
  • Lighting up at a new word or a bug on the sidewalk.
  • Picking a puzzle over a screen, at least sometimes.

If that sounds like your child, the curiosity is already there. Your job is just to protect it.

How can I nurture it at home?

You don't need a curriculum or a Pinterest-perfect playroom. A few things that help:

  • Follow their curiosity. When they ask about dinosaurs, get the dinosaur book. Interest is the best fuel there is.
  • Praise the effort, not the outcome. "You worked so hard to sound that out!" teaches that trying is the win.
  • Read together every day, even for ten minutes. (Here's how to read aloud in a way that builds real skills.)
  • Let them be a little bored. Boredom is where imagination starts.
  • Let them catch you curious. Wonder out loud. Look things up. Enjoy your own book in front of them.

Notice that none of these involve grades. They're about keeping the door to learning open.

When a child pulls away from learning

Sometimes a child who once loved books starts avoiding them. Usually that means the work has gotten ahead of their skills. It rarely means they're "not a reader." When learning feels hard in the wrong way, the joy goes first. That's worth paying attention to. A personalized assessment can show you exactly where the gap is, so reading can feel good again.

Frequently asked questions

Should I never care about grades, then?
You can absolutely care. Just hold them loosely while your child is little. Treat a low score as information, not a verdict. "Let's see what's tricky here" goes a long way.

My child only wants to play, not "learn." Is that a problem?
Not at all. For young children, play is learning. Building a block tower and inventing a story grow real skills. Curiosity in play is exactly what you want to see.
