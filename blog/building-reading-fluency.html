Building Reading Fluency: Helping a Choppy Reader Read Smoothly

By Leslie Dykstra · Certified Early Childhood Specialist · Williamson County, TN

Quick answer: To improve reading fluency, children need repeated practice with the same text, guidance on reading in phrases rather than word by word, and a low-pressure environment where trying sounds natural. Fluency grows through repetition and good modeling — not faster flashcards.

If you've ever sat beside your child while they read aloud, you know the feeling. They work through a sentence one word at a time — "The...cat...sat...on...the...mat." Each word correct, but the whole thing sounds stilted. You're wondering: is this normal? When does it smooth out? How to improve reading fluency is a question I hear often from parents in Brentwood and across Williamson County, and there are real, practical things you can do at home.

As a certified early childhood specialist, I want to start with this: choppy reading at the beginning is completely expected. It means a child is concentrating hard on decoding. The goal is to help decoding become automatic, so the brain can shift its energy toward expression and meaning.

What is reading fluency, exactly?

Fluency is reading that sounds like talking — smooth, appropriately paced, with natural phrasing and expression. A fluent reader doesn't just say each word correctly. They group words into phrases, pause at punctuation, and change their voice when a character is excited or scared.

Fluency sits between decoding and comprehension. A child who's still decoding every word slowly can't give much thought to what the story means. Once decoding becomes automatic and reading sounds more natural, comprehension gets easier too. The two are connected.

What causes choppy reading?

Usually one of these:

  • The text is too hard. When too many words are unfamiliar, a child spends all their energy sounding out and has nothing left for flow.
  • Phonics skills are still developing. Decoding each word takes real effort, and effort looks like pausing.
  • Not enough practice reading the same text twice. Fluency builds through repetition, but many children read something once and move on.
  • Not enough good models. A child who hasn't heard much fluent reading may not know what it's supposed to sound like.

None of these are permanent. All of them respond to practice.

How can I help at home?

Here are the strategies that work best for children ages 5–8.

1. Read the same passage more than once. This is called repeated reading, and it's one of the most well-supported fluency strategies. Have your child read the same short passage three or four times across a few days. The first time, they're decoding. By the third or fourth time, the words are automatic and they can focus on how it sounds. Watch the change — it's genuinely satisfying.

2. Echo reading. You read a sentence or phrase. Your child repeats it back, trying to match your pace and expression. This is a low-pressure way to model fluency without putting a child on the spot.

3. Read it together. Sit side by side and read the same text aloud at the same time. Your child's voice blends with yours. The rhythm of fluent reading gets into their ear. This is called choral reading, and it works especially well for reluctant readers who feel nervous reading alone.

4. Let them perform it. Give a short passage a purpose: "Read it to me like you're the announcer at a game." "Read it to Grandma over the phone." Performing removes some self-consciousness and naturally pushes toward expression.

5. Make sure the book is right-sized. A child should be able to read most of the words in a practice passage on their own. If they're getting stuck on more than one word in ten, the text is probably too hard for fluency practice. (Here's a post on how to choose between decodable books and leveled readers if you're not sure what to pick.)

6. Keep reading aloud to them. Even as your child gets older, hearing you read fluently is one of the best things for their own reading. It builds vocabulary, models expression, and reminds them that books are worth the effort. (How to make read-aloud time count has more on this.)

How long does it take to see improvement?

With regular practice — even ten to fifteen minutes a day — most children show noticeable improvement within a few weeks. The key word is regular. One long session on the weekend doesn't move the needle the way daily short sessions do.

If your child has been working on reading for a while and fluency still isn't coming, it may be worth checking their phonics foundation. Sometimes choppy reading signals that certain decoding skills aren't solid yet. A personalized assessment can tell you exactly where the gap is.

Frequently asked questions

Should I correct my child every time they mispronounce a word?
Not every time — it interrupts the flow and can make reading feel like a test. For fluency practice especially, let small errors go and save corrections for words that come up often. A light "let's try that one again" works better than stopping to drill.

My child reads smoothly to me but their teacher says they're choppy in class. Anxiety plays a big role in fluency. Many children read more freely at home where the stakes feel low. Building confidence at home is genuinely helpful — that comfort often transfers to school over time.

At what age should I be concerned about choppy reading?
Most children are still working on fluency in first grade, and it's normal for that work to continue well into second grade. If a child is reading choppily in third grade despite regular practice, it's worth getting a closer look.
