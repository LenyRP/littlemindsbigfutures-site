Why early math matters

The foundation under every math year to come

Most math struggles in 3rd, 5th, even 8th grade trace back to the same root: a shaky foundation in number sense — the deep, flexible understanding of what numbers actually mean. A child can memorize that 3 + 4 = 7 without ever understanding it. That child will hit a wall; it's only a question of when.

The good news? Ages 3–8 are the perfect window to build that foundation — real understanding, not just counting by rote. Research shows early math skills are one of the strongest predictors of later school success, even predicting future reading ability. When numbers make sense early, everything that follows — addition, subtraction, place value, word problems — clicks into place instead of piling up.

That's what one-on-one math tutoring is for: finding exactly where your child's understanding is solid, exactly where it's wobbly, and building from there — with games, hands-on materials, and the patience of someone who has spent a career helping little minds fall in love with learning.

"Every child is fearfully and wonderfully made — and every child can be a math kid. My job is to help them discover that numbers are something they can trust, play with, and win at."

— Leslie Dykstra, Little Minds, Big Futures

Worth watching for

Signs your child may be struggling with math

Math struggles rarely announce themselves as "I don't understand math." More often they sound and look like this:

  • Counting only works if they start from 1 — asking "what's one more than 6?" stumps them.
  • They count on fingers for the same small facts long after classmates have moved on.
  • Homework that "should take 10 minutes" turns into tears, stalling, or "I'm just bad at math."
  • They can follow a procedure but can't explain why it works — and small changes to a problem throw them completely.
  • They avoid games or activities involving numbers, scores, or money.

If a few of those sound familiar, don't wait for it to resolve itself — math gaps compound quietly. The fix at ages 3–8 is usually simple, playful, and fast compared to the same gap at 12. Try a few everyday math activities at home, and let's talk about whether one-on-one help makes sense.

Math tutoring questions

I work with children ages 3–8. That may sound young for math tutoring, but these are exactly the years when number sense — the deep understanding of what numbers mean — is built.
